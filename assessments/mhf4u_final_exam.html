Question 1 [2 marks]
Which function is a polynomial of degree 4 with negative leading coefficient?
Solution:
Option (a): degree 4, leading coefficient \( -3 < 0 \).
Question 2 [2 marks]
Convert \( 150° \) to radians (exact form).
Solution:
\( 150° \times \frac{\pi}{180°} = \frac{150\pi}{180} = \frac{5\pi}{6} \) rad.
Question 3 [2 marks]
Evaluate \( \log_2 32 + \log_3 \tfrac{1}{27} \).
Solution:
\( \log_2 32 = 5 \) since \( 2^5 = 32 \). \( \log_3 \tfrac{1}{27} = -3 \) since \( 3^{-3}=\tfrac{1}{27} \). Sum \( = 5 + (-3) = 2 \).
Question 4 [2 marks]
State the exact value of \( \cos\!\left(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\right) \).
Solution:
\( \tfrac{7\pi}{6} \) is in Quadrant III; reference angle \( \tfrac{\pi}{6} \). Cosine is negative in Q III, so \( \cos\tfrac{7\pi}{6}=-\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \).
Question 5 [2 marks]
Identify all vertical asymptotes of \( r(x) = \dfrac{2x+3}{x^2 - 4} \).
Solution:
Set denominator to zero: \( x^2-4=0 \Rightarrow x=\pm 2 \). Numerator non-zero at these points. VAs: \( x=2 \) and \( x=-2 \).
Question 6 [2 marks]
For \( f(x)=2x-1 \) and \( g(x)=x^2 \), evaluate \( (f \circ g)(3) \).
Solution:
\( g(3)=9 \), then \( f(9)=2(9)-1=17 \).
Question 7 [3 marks]
Solve for \( x \): \( 5^{2x-1} = 125 \).
Solution:
\( 125 = 5^3 \), so \( 2x-1=3 \Rightarrow x=2 \).
Question 8 [2 marks]
Identify the amplitude and period of \( y = -4\cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}x\right) + 1 \).
Solution:
Amplitude \( = |-4| = 4 \). Period \( = \dfrac{2\pi}{\pi/2} = 4 \).

Part A — Knowledge & Understanding

Q1 [2] — Answer: (a)

(a) \( -3x^4+2x^2-1 \): degree 4, leading coefficient \( -3<0 \). ✓

Q2 [2] — Answer: \( \tfrac{5\pi}{6} \) rad

\( 150°\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{180°}=\tfrac{150\pi}{180}=\tfrac{5\pi}{6} \).

Q3 [2] — Answer: 2

\( \log_2 32=5 \); \( \log_3 \tfrac{1}{27}=\log_3 3^{-3}=-3 \); sum \( =2 \).

Q4 [2] — Answer: (b) \( -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \)

Q III, reference \( \tfrac{\pi}{6} \), cosine negative → \( -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \).

Q5 [2] — Answer: \( x=2,\ x=-2 \)

Factor denominator: \( (x-2)(x+2) \). Numerator \( 2x+3 \) is non-zero at \( x=\pm 2 \), so both are vertical asymptotes.

Q6 [2] — Answer: 17

\( g(3)=9 \); \( f(9)=2(9)-1=17 \).

Q7 [3] — Answer: \( x=2 \)

\( 5^{2x-1}=5^3 \Rightarrow 2x-1=3 \Rightarrow x=2 \).

Q8 [2] — Amplitude 4, Period 4

\( |a|=4 \); period \( =\tfrac{2\pi}{|k|}=\tfrac{2\pi}{\pi/2}=4 \).

Q9 [3] — Odd

\( f(-x)=(-x)^5-3(-x)^3+(-x)=-x^5+3x^3-x=-(x^5-3x^3+x)=-f(x) \). So odd. (1 for substitution, 1 for simplification, 1 for conclusion.)

Q10 [2] — \( \log_{10}\!\left(\tfrac{x^2}{x+1}\right) \)

Power law: \( 2\log x=\log x^2 \). Quotient law: \( \log x^2-\log(x+1)=\log\!\tfrac{x^2}{x+1} \).

Q11 [3] — \( f(x)=2x(x+2)(x-3) \)

Factored form: \( f(x)=a x(x+2)(x-3) \). Use \( f(1)=-12 \): \( a(1)(3)(-2)=-6a=-12 \Rightarrow a=2 \).

Part B — Thinking & Inquiry

Q12 [5] — \( x \in (-3,2) \cup (5,\infty) \)

Test \( x=2 \): \( 8-16-22+30=0 \). Factor out \( (x-2) \): \( x^3-4x^2-11x+30=(x-2)(x^2-2x-15)=(x-2)(x-5)(x+3) \). Sign chart with critical points \( -3, 2, 5 \): the cubic with positive leading coefficient is positive on \( (-3,2)\cup(5,\infty) \).

Q13 [5] — Identity Proven

Using \( 1-\cos 2\theta=2\sin^2\theta \) and \( \sin 2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta \):
\( \dfrac{2\sin^2\theta}{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}=\tan\theta \). ✓
Restrictions: \( \sin\theta\neq 0 \) and \( \cos\theta\neq 0 \), i.e. \( \theta\neq \tfrac{n\pi}{2},\ n\in\mathbb{Z} \).

Q14 [5] — \( x = \tfrac{\pi}{6}, \tfrac{5\pi}{6}, \tfrac{\pi}{2} \)

Let \( u=\sin x \): \( 2u^2-3u+1=0 \Rightarrow (2u-1)(u-1)=0 \Rightarrow u=\tfrac{1}{2} \) or \( u=1 \). For \( \sin x=\tfrac{1}{2} \) on \( [0,2\pi) \): \( x=\tfrac{\pi}{6}, \tfrac{5\pi}{6} \). For \( \sin x=1 \): \( x=\tfrac{\pi}{2} \).

Q15 [5] — \( x=5 \)

Combine: \( \log_2[(x+3)(x-3)]=4 \Rightarrow (x+3)(x-3)=2^4=16 \Rightarrow x^2-9=16 \Rightarrow x^2=25 \Rightarrow x=\pm 5 \). Restrictions: \( x+3>0 \) and \( x-3>0 \Rightarrow x>3 \). Reject \( x=-5 \). Therefore \( x=5 \).

Q16 [5] — \( (f\circ g)(x)=x+4 \), domain \( x\ge -5 \), \( x=4 \)

\( (f\circ g)(x)=f(\sqrt{x+5})=(\sqrt{x+5})^2-1=x+5-1=x+4 \). Domain inherits from \( g \): \( x+5\ge 0 \Rightarrow x\ge -5 \). Set \( x+4=8\Rightarrow x=4 \) (in domain ✓).

Part C — Communication

Q17 [5] — Polynomial Sketch

Leading term \( -\tfrac{1}{2}x^4 \) → both ends to \( -\infty \). x-intercepts: \( -3 \) (cross, order 1), \( 1 \) (bounce, order 2), \( 4 \) (cross, order 1). y-intercept: \( f(0)=-\tfrac12(3)(1)(-4)=6 \). Sketch: enters from below at left, crosses at \( -3 \), rises through \( (0,6) \), bounces off x-axis at \( 1 \), descends, crosses at \( 4 \), exits below.

Q18 [5] — Sinusoidal Sketch

Amplitude \( =3 \). Period \( =\tfrac{2\pi}{2}=\pi \). Phase shift: \( \tfrac{\pi}{4} \) right. Vertical translation: \( -1 \) (midline \( y=-1 \)). Max \( =-1+3=2 \); Min \( =-1-3=-4 \). One zero: solve \( 3\sin(2(x-\tfrac{\pi}{4}))=1 \) — example zero at \( x=\tfrac{\pi}{4} \) where the curve crosses \( y=-1 \) (midline) ascending.

Q19 [4] — AROC vs IROC

AROC over \( [a,b] \) = \( \tfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \), the slope of the secant line through \( (a,f(a)) \) and \( (b,f(b)) \). IROC at \( x=a \) is the limiting slope of secants as the second point approaches \( a \); geometrically the slope of the tangent line at \( (a,f(a)) \). Diagram: curve with secant chord (AROC) and tangent line touching at one point (IROC).

Q20 [4] — Logarithm Error

Error: \( \log(a+b)\neq \log a+\log b \). The correct product law is \( \log(ab)=\log a+\log b \). Counterexample: \( \log_{10}(2+8)=\log_{10}10=1 \), but \( \log_{10}2+\log_{10}8=\log_{10}16\approx 1.204 \neq 1 \).

Q21 [4] — Removable Discontinuity

\( r(x)=\tfrac{x^2-9}{x-3}=\tfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}=x+3 \) for \( x\neq 3 \). The factor \( (x-3) \) cancels — both numerator and denominator are zero at \( x=3 \). Because the simplified function is defined and continuous, \( x=3 \) is a hole rather than a VA. Hole at \( (3,6) \).

Q22 [3] — Growth Comparison

Exponential \( b^x \) (with \( b>1 \)) increases by a constant factor over each unit interval, doubling/tripling repeatedly, while a polynomial \( x^n \) increases by amounts that grow only as a polynomial of \( x \). For sufficiently large \( x \), \( b^x \) exceeds any polynomial. Logarithmic functions are the inverse — they grow slower than any positive power of \( x \).
